# SA 620: Using the Work of an Auditor's Expert

## Objective
- 🎯 To determine whether to use an expert (Para 5a)
- 🎯 If using, determine if work is adequate (Para 5b)

## Scope
- Applies to: Work in non-accounting/auditing fields (Para 1)
- Does NOT apply to: Team member with specialised accounting/auditing expertise (Para 2a)
  - 🔗 See SA 220
- Does NOT apply to: Management's expert used by entity (Para 2b)
  - 🔗 See SA 500
- Sole responsibility: Auditor alone is responsible for opinion (Para 3)
- Effective from: April 1, 2010 (Para 4)

## Definitions
- 📌 Auditor’s expert (Para 6a): An individual or organisation possessing expertise in a field other than accounting or auditing, whose work in that field is used by the auditor to assist the auditor in obtaining sufficient appropriate audit evidence. An auditor’s expert may be either an auditor’s internal expert (who is a partner or staff, including temporary staff, of the auditor’s firm or a network firm), or an auditor’s external expert.
- 📌 Expertise (Para 6b): Skills, knowledge and experience in a particular field.
- 📌 Management’s expert (Para 6c): An individual or organisation possessing expertise in a field other than accounting or auditing, whose work in that field is used by the entity to assist the entity in preparing the financial statements.

## Requirements
- Determining the Need
  - 🎯 The auditor shall determine whether to use expert work (Para 7)
    - ⚠️ If expertise in non-accounting field is necessary
- Nature, Timing and Extent
  - 🎯 The auditor shall consider matters including: (Para 8)
    - Nature of matter relating to expert's work
    - Risks of material misstatement in that matter
    - Significance of expert's work in audit context
    - Auditor's previous experience with that expert
    - Whether expert is subject to firm QC policies
- Competence and Objectivity
  - 🎯 The auditor shall evaluate competence, capabilities and objectivity (Para 9)
    - ⚠️ If external expert: inquiry regarding objectivity threats required
- Understanding Field of Expertise
  - 🎯 The auditor shall obtain sufficient understanding to: (Para 10)
    - Determine nature, scope and objectives of work
    - Evaluate adequacy of work for audit purposes
- Agreement with Expert
  - 🎯 The auditor shall agree in writing when appropriate: (Para 11)
    - Nature, scope and objectives of work
    - Respective roles and responsibilities
    - Nature, timing and extent of communication
    - Need to observe confidentiality requirements
- Evaluating Adequacy
  - 🎯 The auditor shall evaluate adequacy of work including: (Para 12)
    - Relevance and reasonableness of findings or conclusions
    - ⚠️ If using significant assumptions/methods: evaluate their reasonableness
    - ⚠️ If using significant source data: evaluate completeness and accuracy
- Inadequate Work
  - 🎯 If work is inadequate, the auditor shall: (Para 13)
    - Agree on further work to be performed; or
    - Perform further audit procedures appropriate to circumstances
- Reference in Audit Report
  - 🎯 ⚠️ The auditor shall not refer to expert in unmodified opinion (Para 14)
    - ⚠️ Unless required by law or regulation to do so
      - 🎯 If required, shall indicate responsibility is not reduced
  - 🎯 ⚠️ If referencing expert to explain modified opinion (Para 15)
    - 🎯 Shall indicate reference does not reduce auditor responsibility

## Application Guidance
- Explains Definitions
  - Distinguishing accounting from other expertise requires judgment (Para A2)
  - Both personal and organisational attributes are relevant (Para A3)
- Explains Determining the Need
  - Assists in risk assessment and audit responses (Para A4)
  - Management lacking expertise increases misstatement risks (Para A5)
  - Auditor must ensure team has collective competence (Para A6)
  - Auditor may obtain understanding without an expert (Para A7)
  - Decision factors for using an auditor's expert (Para A8)
  - Factors if management used an expert (Para A9)
- Explains Nature, Timing and Extent
  - Subjective matters require more extensive procedures (Para A10)
  - Internal experts are subject to SQC 1 policies (Para A11)
  - External experts are not subject to SQC 1 (Para A12)
  - Engagement team may rely on firm QC system (Para A13)
- Explains Competence and Objectivity
  - Capability includes location, time, and resources (Para A14)
  - Multiple sources inform auditor about expert competence (Para A15)
  - Professional standards and legal requirements are relevant (Para A16)
  - Specialized areas within a field require specific competence (Para A17)
  - Broad range of circumstances may threaten objectivity (Para A18)
  - Safeguards may not always reduce threats acceptably (Para A19)
  - External expert objectivity requires inquiry and discussion (Para A20)
- Explains Understanding Field of Expertise
  - Obtain understanding via discussion or past experience (Para A21)
  - Relevant aspects include specific specialties and standards (Para A22)
- Explains Agreement with Expert
  - Agreement required for both internal and external experts (Para A23)
  - Certain factors suggest need for detailed written agreement (Para A24)
  - External expert agreement often uses engagement letter (Para A25)
  - Unwritten agreements evidenced by memos or firm policies (Para A26)
  - Discuss relevant technical performance standards (Para A27)
  - Agreement includes testing source data and report usage (Para A28)
  - External expert working papers are their own property (Para A29)
  - Effective two-way communication facilitates proper integration (Para A30)
  - Ethical confidentiality provisions apply to experts (Para A31)
- Explains Evaluating Adequacy
  - Evaluation scope affected by expert's competence and familiarity (Para A32)
  - Specific procedures required to evaluate findings (Para A33)
  - Standardized presentation and clear expression indicate reasonableness (Para A34)
  - Focus on how expert reviewed management assumptions (Para A35)
  - SA 540 assists evaluating specialized entity models (Para A36)
  - Assumptions should be generally accepted and consistent (Para A37)
  - Source data requires verification of origin and completeness (Para A38)
  - Auditor may supervise expert's testing of technical data (Para A39)
- Explains Inadequate Work
  - Unresolved inadequacy may require modified audit opinion (Para A40)
- Explains Reference in Audit Report
  - Public sector transparency may require expert reference (Para A41)
  - Permission from expert may be needed before referencing (Para A42)

## Key Nuances and Exceptions
- ⚠️ Para 2: Excludes specialized audit team members and management's experts
- ⚠️ Para 9: External experts require specific inquiry regarding objectivity threats
- ⚠️ Para 12: Significant assumptions require separate relevance and reasonableness evaluation
- ⚠️ Para 14: Never refer to expert in unmodified opinion unless law requires
- ⚠️ Para 15: Referencing expert for modified opinion must disclaim reduced responsibility

## ICAI Listed Examples
- Para A1 — Examples of non-accounting expertise
  - 📋 Valuation of complex financial instruments, land and buildings
  - 📋 Valuation of plant and machinery, jewelry, works of art
  - 📋 Valuation of intangible assets or impaired assets
  - 📋 Actuarial calculation for insurance or employee benefits
  - 📋 Estimation of oil and gas reserves
  - 📋 Valuation of environmental liabilities and site clean-up costs
  - 📋 Interpretation of contracts, laws and regulations
  - 📋 Analysis of complex or unusual tax compliance issues
- Para A4 — Examples of areas expert assists auditor
  - 📋 Obtaining understanding of entity and environment
  - 📋 Identifying and assessing risks of material misstatement
  - 📋 Determining overall responses to assessed risks
  - 📋 Designing and performing further audit procedures
  - 📋 Evaluating sufficiency and appropriateness of audit evidence
- Para A7 — Examples of gaining understanding without expert
  - 📋 Experience in auditing entities requiring such expertise
  - 📋 Education or professional development in the field
  - 📋 Discussion with auditors who performed similar engagements
- Para A9 — Examples of factors if management used expert
  - 📋 Nature, scope and objectives of management expert's work
  - 📋 Whether expert is employed or engaged by entity
  - 📋 Extent of management control over expert's work
  - 📋 Management expert's competence and capabilities
  - 📋 Whether expert is subject to technical performance standards
  - 📋 Controls within entity over management expert's work
- Para A10 — Examples suggesting more extensive procedures
  - 📋 Work relates to subjective and complex judgments
  - 📋 Auditor has not previously used this expert's work
  - 📋 Expert performing procedures integral to the audit
  - 📋 Expert is external and not subject to firm QC
- Para A15 — Examples of sources of competence information
  - 📋 Personal experience with previous work of expert
  - 📋 Discussions with that expert
  - 📋 Discussions with other auditors familiar with expert
  - 📋 Knowledge of expert's qualifications or industry membership
  - 📋 Published papers or books written by expert
  - 📋 Auditor's firm's quality control policies and procedures
- Para A20 — Examples of relevant interests and relationships
  - 📋 Financial interests
  - 📋 Business and personal relationships
  - 📋 Provision of other services by the expert
- Para A24 — Examples suggesting detailed written agreement
  - 📋 Expert will have access to sensitive confidential information
  - 📋 Respective roles differ from those normally expected
  - 📋 Multi-jurisdictional legal or regulatory requirements apply
  - 📋 Matter relating to expert's work is highly complex
  - 📋 Auditor has not previously used this expert
  - 📋 Greater extent and significance of expert's work
- Para A26 — Examples of unwritten agreement evidence
  - 📋 Planning memoranda or related audit program papers
  - 📋 Policies and procedures of the auditor's firm
- Para A33 — Examples of specific procedures to evaluate adequacy
  - 📋 Inquiries of the auditor's expert
  - 📋 Reviewing expert's working papers and reports
  - 📋 Observing the auditor's expert's work
  - 📋 Examining published statistical reports from reputable sources
  - 📋 Confirming relevant matters with third parties
  - 📋 Performing detailed analytical procedures
  - 📋 Re-performing calculations
  - 📋 Discussion with another expert with relevant expertise
  - 📋 Discussing auditor's expert's report with management
- Para A34 — Examples of evaluating relevance and reasonableness
  - 📋 Presented consistent with expert's profession standards
  - 📋 Clearly expressed referencing agreed objectives and scope
  - 📋 Based on appropriate period considering subsequent events
  - 📋 Subject to any reservation, limitation or restriction
  - 📋 Based on appropriate consideration of errors or deviations
- Para A37 — Examples of evaluating significant assumptions
  - 📋 Generally accepted within auditor's expert's field
  - 📋 Consistent with applicable financial reporting framework
  - 📋 Dependent on the use of specialised models
  - 📋 Consistent with management assumptions
- Para A38 — Examples of procedures testing source data
  - 📋 Verifying origin of data and relevant internal controls
  - 📋 Reviewing data for completeness and internal consistency

## Logical Flow Chains
- Determining Need for Expert
  - ⚠️ If non-accounting expertise is required (Para 7)
    - leads to: Evaluate expert competence and objectivity (Para 9)
    - leads to: Obtain understanding of expert field (Para 10)
    - leads to: Agree on terms and scope (Para 11)
- Handling Inadequate Expert Work
  - Evaluate adequacy of expert work (Para 12)
    - ⚠️ if inadequate: Agree on further work by expert (Para 13)
    - or: Perform further audit procedures (Para 13)
      - ⚠️ if still unresolved: Modify audit opinion (Para A40)
