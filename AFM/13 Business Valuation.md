# Business Valuation

## Conceptual Framework of Valuation
- Basic purpose of any enterprise is to earn profits to sustain itself and promote growth
- Corporate valuation can be traced back to the United East India Company (Dutch East India Company) which was the first corporation valued for an IPO
- Valuation is both a science and an art and is an inseparable part of strategic financial management
- Valuation needs are typical for several purposes:
  - Information for internal stakeholders
  - Comparison with similar enterprises for understanding management efficiency
  - Future public listing of the enterprise
  - Strategic planning like finding value drivers or correct deployment of surplus cash
  - Ball park price for acquisition

## Important Terms Associated with Valuation

### Present Value of Cash Flows
- Based on the concept of Time Value of Money
- 📎 Example Insight: A receipt of 1000 twelve months hence is not the same as today and at a rate of 10% the discounted value is approximately 909

### Internal Rate of Return (IRR)
- Discount rate that equates the present value of cash inflows to the present value of cash outflows making NPV zero
- Also visualized as the discount rate that gets the present values of cash inflows equal to the investment
- Decision Rule: The higher the IRR of a project the more likely it gets selected for further investments

### Return on Investment (ROI)
- Return over the investment made in an entity from a stakeholder's point of view
- Calculated as return divided by the investment cost
- 📎 Example Insight: A stakeholder sells shares valued at 1400 that were initially invested at 1000 making the ROI equal to 40% for this standalone transaction
- ⚠️ Nuance: If shares are held for several years the stakeholder calculates ROI by taking into account the time value of money which adjusts the ROI over the holding period
- 📎 Example Insight: If shares worth 1000 are held at the start and an additional 200 is invested during the year and the ending value is 2000 the returns are 800 and the average investment is 1100 yielding an ROI of 72%
- 📌 Note: Average cost of investments is used to recognize the timing of the investment and stakeholders with multiple portfolios use this average cost
- Entity Perspective: ROI is used to calculate returns generated by individual or incremental investments on a project to make further decisions
- ⚠️ Nuance: ROI is a historical ratio so decisions based on it can only be course-corrective or used for channeling further investments into successful business lines

### Perpetual Growth Rate (Gordon Model)
- Assumes a perpetual growth in dividend where potential investors take the latest dividend payout and factor it with expected rate of return
- ❌ Not suitable when: Potential investors need a model that factors in all complex real-world parameters or when dividends do not grow perpetually at a steady rate
- 📌 Note: This model is highly preferred by academicians as it neatly fits into a constant rate model for deliberation purposes

### Terminal Value (TV)
- Terminal refers to the end of an investment or line of business where an investor decides to pull out and book profits
- 📌 Note: Terminal Value is also referred to as Horizon Value which the investor forecasts for valuing an investment at the exit point
- ⚠️ Nuance: Terminal Value is mostly estimated using a perpetual growth model as per the Gordon model

## Approaches and Methods of Valuation
- Valuation in Mergers and Acquisitions is used to gauge the price to be offered to the target company
- ⚠️ Nuance: If the target company is unlisted the price of acquisition is at a negotiated price acceptable by both companies
- ⚠️ Nuance: For listed companies the stock market value or market capitalization can form the basis of valuation
- ⚠️ Nuance: Stock market price is a guide to the acquiring company but does not give an absolute estimate of worth since it depends on Market Efficiency
- ⚠️ Nuance: If only a small portion of total shares is quoted the market price represents a marginal portion of overall capital requiring further analysis
- Broadly there are three approaches to value an enterprise:
  - Assets Based Valuation Model
  - Earning Based Models
  - Cash Flow Based Models

### Asset Based Approach
- Value of shares of the target company is computed in terms of net assets acquired and is not based on income generation but on income-generating assets
- ❌ Not suitable when: Valuing IT companies where hard assets make little importance as their assets are intellectual property rights and human resources
- Net Asset Value Method:
  - Also called the Book Value Method
  - 🔢 Formula: Net Fixed Asset = Fixed Assets + Net Current Assets - Long Term Debt
  - ⚠️ Limitation: Uses historical costs which are easily available but has little relevance as the Balance Sheet is not a valuation device
  - ✅ Implication: This method offers a lower limit to value the shares of the target company
  - ⚠️ Limitation: Ignores the current asset valuation even for intangible assets such as Brand and Intellectual Property Rights
- Net Realizable Value Method:
  - Also called Liquidation Value or Adjusted Book Value
  - Defined as the realizable value of all assets after deduction of liquidation expenses and paying off liabilities
  - ⚠️ Nuance: Liquidation expenses can be ignored if the business of the target company is acquired as a going concern
  - ⚠️ Limitation: Calculating net realizable value is complex as the buyer in an off-market purchase is likely to offer the lowest prices
  - ❌ Not suitable when: A company wants a popular day-to-day valuation because it involves total break up of the target company
  - ✅ Implication: Use this method where the acquirer is interested in selling one part of the business and integrating the remaining part with existing operations
  - 📎 Example Insight: In a liquidation scenario net assets with a book value of 1,00,000 are realized at 1,05,000 which for 5,000 equity shares results in a share value of 21
- Replaceable Value Method:
  - Involves valuation based on determining the cost of a group of assets and liabilities of an equivalent company in the open market
  - 📌 Note: This has an advantage over Book Value because it takes into consideration proper valuation and is generally higher than Net Realizable Value since quick asset disposal is not encouraged
  - 📌 Note: Many authors believe this represents the maximum price that an acquirer would pay for the equivalent business
  - ⚠️ Limitation: Only hard assets are taken into consideration and the loyalty of the staff cannot be factored in
  - Asset-Based Approach General Drawback: It fails to consider the ability of the enterprise to generate future revenues and how market dynamics affect future operations and cash flows

### Income Based Approach
- Overcomes the drawbacks of the asset-backed approach by referring to the earning potential
- 📌 Note: This method is more suitable when the acquiring company intends to continue the business of the target company for the foreseeable future without selling or liquidating assets
- 📌 Note: Any additional earning due to the acquisition (synergy) must also be considered in the valuation
- PE Ratio or Earning Yield Multiplier:
  - Generally used for valuing listed companies whose PE Ratios are available and takes into account expected growth rate and market expectations
  - 🔢 Formula: Price Per Share = EPS * PE Ratio
  - ⚠️ Nuance: For unlisted companies the PE Ratio of equivalent companies or the industry can be used after making downward adjustments for additional risk due to non-listing
  - Unlisted Company Steps:
    - Step 1: Choosing PE Ratio of equivalent quoted company
    - Step 2: Making adjustment downward for additional risk due to non-listing of shares
    - Step 3: Determination of future maintainable EPS
    - Step 4: Multiply same EPS with adjusted PE Ratio
- Capitalisation of Earning Method:
  - Value of business is calculated by capitalization of expected annual maintainable profit using an appropriate required rate of return or yield or discounting rate
  - 📌 Note: Annual expected maintainable profit is calculated using the weighted average of previous years' profits after adjusting for synergy benefits or economies of scale
  - 🔢 Formula: Required Earning Yield = EPS / Share Price
  - 🔢 Formula: Reciprocal of PE Ratio = 1 / PE Ratio
  - 🔢 Formula: Capitalized Earning Value = Expected Annual Maintainable Profit / Capitalization Rate or Required Earning Yield
  - 📌 Note: This is a forward-looking approach which is its main advantage
  - ⚠️ Limitation: It is difficult to estimate expected future profit and there are differences in the treatment of extraordinary and exceptional items

### Cash Flow Based Approach (DCF)
- Takes into account the quantum of free cash available in future periods and discounts it to match the flow's risk
- Overcomes the problem of over-reliance on historical data seen in asset and income-based methods
- Decision Rule: If the present value arrived at is more than the current cost of investment the valuation is attractive to stakeholders and analysts
- Five Steps in DCF:
  - Step a: Arriving at the Free Cash Flows
  - Step b: Forecasting of future cash flows
  - Step c: Determining the discount rate based on the cost of capital
  - Step d: Finding out the Terminal Value of the enterprise
  - Step e: Finding out the present values of both the free cash flows and the TV and interpreting results
- 📎 Example Insight: Step a is done by taking EAT (600) subtracting one-time incomes (200) adding one-time expenses (100) and adding depreciation (100) to arrive at Free Cash Flow of 600
- 📎 Example Insight: Step b adjusts Free Cash Flow for changes in Working Capital and investments in Fixed Assets to arrive at Adjusted Free Cash Flow
- 📎 Example Insight: Step c applies PVF at WACC (8%) to get the Present Value of Cash Flow
- 📎 Example Insight: Step d calculates TV using the perpetual growth rate (3%) where TV = (CF at Year 3 * growth rate) / (WACC - growth rate) yielding 10656.17
- 📎 Example Insight: Step e calculates Total DCF of enterprise by adding the PV of cash flows to the TV arrived which yields 12,107.81 thousands
- ⚠️ Limitation: The entire calculation gravitates heavily on the WACC and the TV where TV is often a significant portion of final value meaning growth rate and underlying assumptions must be thoroughly validated

## Measuring Cost of Equity

### Capital Assets Pricing Model (CAPM)
- Views returns as a direct benefit of assuming risks
- 🔢 Formula: R = rf + β (rm - rf)
- Where R is expected rate of return, rf is risk free rate of return, β is Beta value of the stock, and rm is market rate of return

### Arbitrage Pricing Model (APT)
- Introduced in the 1970s by Mr. Stephen Alan Ross to address the oversimplification of risks in CAPM
- Breaks down risks into individual identifiable macro-economic elements called factors and assigns a specific beta to each factor
- 🔢 Formula: Return = Rf + β1(RP1) + β2(RP2) + ... + βj(RPn)
- ⚠️ Limitation: APT by itself does not provide the macro-economic factors that need to be tested for sensitivity and these must be developed judicially by financial analysts

### Estimating Beta and Valuation of Unlisted Companies
- Asset-based, earnings-based, and cash flow-based approaches can be applied freely for publicly traded companies where financial information is readily available
- Valuation of unlisted/privately held companies is an onerous task due to the absence of publicly available information
- Arriving at the Cost of Capital for private firms is difficult because they may have zero or insignificant amounts of debt compared to listed companies
- ⚠️ Nuance: Since strategic investors look to grow the private firm on leveraged funds going forward the valuation date beta must reflect the unleveraged position using unlevered beta as opposed to levered beta
- ⚠️ Nuance: An existing listed company investing in a brand new line of business should not use its WACC but should assess the WACC using Asset Beta (Ungeared Beta) adjusted according to its own gearing level
- 📌 Note: Asset Beta represents only systematic risk of the underlying project or asset of the company and does not represent any financial risk
- Asset Beta calculation:
  - 🔢 Formula: βa = βe * [ E / (E + D(1-t)) ] + βd * [ D(1-t) / (E + D(1-t)) ]
  - Where βa is Ungeared or Asset Beta, βe is Geared or Equity Beta, βd is Debt Beta, E is Equity, D is Debt, and t is Tax Rate
  - 📌 Note: Company's Equity Beta shall always be greater than Asset Beta and if the company is debt free then Equity Beta equals Asset Beta
  - ⚠️ Nuance: Generally assumed that Debt Beta tends to be Zero as bond returns are not linked to market volatility simplifying the equation to βa = βe * [ E / (E + D(1-t)) ]
- Steps to compute Equity Beta for a new business or project:
  - Step 1: Identify Pure Play firms (proxy companies) engaged entirely in the same business and get their Equity Betas
  - Step 2: De-gear the Equity Betas of proxy companies to compute Asset Beta because different companies have different gearing levels
  - Step 3: If only one proxy company continue with its Asset Beta and if multiple take the average of Asset Betas or opt for the most appropriate one
  - Step 4: Re-gear the Asset Beta as per the capital structure of the appraising company to reflect financial risk using the formula βe = βa * [ (E + D(1-t)) / E ]
  - Step 5: Insert computed βe in CAPM to calculate required rate of return or value of the business

## Relative Valuation
- Method to arrive at a relative value using comparative analysis to peers or similar enterprises
- 📌 Note: Contemporary analysts use relative valuation in conjunction with intrinsic approaches to validate the intrinsic value arrived earlier
- ⚠️ Nuance: The more the asset pricing gets correlated to similar assets in the market the more inclusive and credible it gets
- Relative Valuation Steps:
  - 1. Find out the drivers that will be the best representative for deriving at the multiple
  - 2. Determine the results based on the chosen drivers through financial ratios
  - 3. Find out the comparable firms and perform the comparative analysis
  - 4. Iterate the value of the firm obtained to smoothen out the deviations
- Step 1 Drivers:
  - (a) Enterprise value based multiples: EV/EBITDA, EV/Invested Capital, and EV/Sales
    - ⚠️ Nuance: EV/Invested Capital is more appropriate for capital intensive enterprises
    - ⚠️ Nuance: EV/Sales is used by companies that are cash rich, have a huge order book, and forecast organic growth through own capital
  - (b) Equity value based multiples: P/E ratio and Price Earning Growth (PEG) Ratio
    - ⚠️ Nuance: PEG is more suitable for relative valuation of high growth or sunrise industries
- Step 2 Financial Ratios:
  - Choosing the right financial ratio is vital and a factor-based approach helps
  - 📎 Example Insight: A firm generating revenue mostly from exports is highly influenced by FX fluctuations where a pure P/E ratio may not reflect this reality
  - ❌ Not suitable when: EV/Invested Capital is used for a company light on core assets or with significant investment properties
- Step 3 Comparable Firms:
  - Challenge of arriving at the right mix because no two entities can be identical
  - 📎 Example Insight: Software company X and Y are similar in size and capital structure but X derives revenue from dedicated service contracts (FTE pricing) and Y earns through Unit Transfer Pricing (UTP) which dramatically changes the risk structure
  - 📌 Note: Take benchmarks with a pinch of salt
  - ⚠️ Nuance: If a firm operates in a niche market finding comparable firms is difficult and analysts must look beyond the current operating market to other industries or perform a regression to find metrics
- Inherent Assumptions of Relative Valuation:
  - i. The market is efficient
  - ii. The function between the fundamentals and the multiples is linear
  - iii. The firms that are comparable are similar in structure, risk, and growth pattern
- Approaches to Enterprise Value (EV):
  - (a) Take Entity Value as the base and adjust for debt values for arriving at EV
  - (b) Take a balance sheet based approach and arrive at EV
- 📎 Example Insight: Illustration 2 calculates per share earning value using CAPM capitalization rate of 11.25% and equity value of 3200k using an EBITDA multiple of 5
- 📎 Example Insight: Illustration 3 uses the balance sheet approach to calculate EV of 5,40,000 using market capitalization of 8,40,000 adding debt of 2,00,000 and subtracting cash of 5,00,000

## Other Approaches to Value Measurement

### Contemporary Approaches to Valuation
- Business paradigm shift due to technology-driven operations where analysts use PEs, Exit Multiples, LBOs, and Brand Value
- Internet companies have virtually zero fixed assets but robust online presence and brand recall
- Valuation methods for internet companies: Price Per Page visited or Price Per Subscriber
- ⚠️ Nuance: Discerning investors still ask for cash to sales ratio and apply a DCF model before putting money in
- Goodwill-based approach:
  - 📎 Example Insight: A retail giant acquiring a mom-and-pop store first takes an asset-based valuation and then values goodwill separately by linking a multiple to annual sales or footfall
- Price Earning Ratio (PER):
  - Equates EPS to prevailing stock market price
  - 📌 Note: PER is a relative figure and comparison across industries in the same sector gives a more acceptable median PER
- Leveraged Buy Outs (LBOs):
  - Private Equity firms acquire enterprises in the rough using a clever mix of debt and equity (typically 70:30) targeting to sell within 3 to 5 years
  - 📎 Example Insight: VC buys software company X for 250 Lacs (including 100% premium and 50 Lacs goodwill) gets a banker to pump in 200 Lacs for expansion and exits in Year 3 using an exit multiple of 7 times EBDAT yielding an equity value of 684 Lacs
  - ⚠️ Limitation: Future cannot be predicted with exactitude and if the industry is caught in a cyclical slowdown the VC is saddled with huge loans and interest

### Chop-Shop Method
- Identifies multi-industry companies that are undervalued and would have more value if separated to buy assets below replacement value
- Chop-Shop Steps:
  - Step 1: Identify firm's business segments and calculate average capitalization ratios for firms in those industries
  - Step 2: Calculate a theoretical market value based upon each of the average capitalization ratios
  - Step 3: Average the theoretical market values to determine the chop-shop value of the firm
- 📎 Example Insight: Illustration 4 computes Cornett GMBH's break-up valuation based on Capital-to-Sales, Capital-to-Assets, and Capital-to-Operating Income to find an average theoretical value of 4,178,000

### Economic Value Added (EVA)
- Holistic method of evaluating financial performance and economic contribution to society
- Wealth is generated only if there is a creation of returns in excess of cost of capital invested
- 📌 Note: EVA insists on separation of the firm's operations from its financing
- Why EVA: Highlights performance of management and makes them accountable for individual decisions unlike simple number-crunching metrics (ROI, ROCE)
- 🔢 Formula: EVA = NOPAT – (Invested Capital * WACC)  or  EVA = NOPAT – Capital Charge
- NOPAT is Net Operating Profit After Tax (EBIT minus tax expense)
- 📌 Note: Depreciation is left untouched in NOPAT as it is an operational expense for EVA purposes
- ⚠️ Nuance: Adjust non-cash expenses like provision for bad and doubtful debts to NOPAT to arrive at actual cash earnings
- Invested Capital is total assets minus non-interest bearing current liabilities (sundry creditors, billing in advance) adjusted for non-cash elements
- 📎 Example Insight: Illustration 5 calculates EVA of A Ltd by computing NOPAT of 307, Invested Capital of 920, WACC of 8.44%, and subtracting Capital Charge of 77.65 to yield EVA of 229.35

### Market Value Added (MVA)
- MVA is the current market value of the firm minus the invested capital
- 📌 Note: MVA attempts to resolve issues in EVA like ignoring value drivers and book value and gauges performance from a market capitalization point of view
- ⚠️ Nuance: In the short term EVA may have a slightly negative correlation compared to MVA (such as a stock going ex-dividend) but the gap gets closed over time
- Main objective of EVA is to show management efficiency in generating returns over the hurdle rate of invested capital

### Shareholder Value Analysis (SVA)
- Why SVA: NOPAT in EVA is a historical figure and does not capture future earnings, cash flows, or future investment opportunities. SVA plugs this gap using value drivers (earnings potential in terms of sales, investment opportunities, cost of incremental capital)
- SVA Steps:
  - (a) Arrive at Future Cash Flows (FCFs) by using a mix of value drivers
  - (b) Discount these FCFs using the WACC
  - (c) Add the terminal value to the present values of FCFs
  - (d) Add current market value of non-core assets and marketable investments
  - (e) Reduce the value of debt from the result to arrive at value of equity
  - 📎 Example Insight: Calculations show operating cash flows adjusted for capital investments and working capital discounted at 12% with a TV multiplier of 8.33 and adjustments for investment property and debt to find the value of equity of 498.81

## Arriving at Fair Value
- Ultimate purpose of a potential acquirer is to purchase a controlling stake at a fair price
- 📌 Note: Chartered accountants view fair value as an arm's length transaction, financial analysts view it as PV in cash terms, and speculative investors view it as arbitrage opportunities
- ⚠️ Nuance: In upward boom times valuations defy fair value (like the dot-com boom) and companies vanish when downturns arrive
- 📌 Note: There is no single correct method of valuation and it is better to determine a range of values representing minimum acceptable by seller and maximum payable by buyer
- 🎯 Examiner Focus: To solve valuation questions:
  - (i) Unless specified otherwise calculate valuation by as many methods as possible with available data
  - (ii) Give comments on the valuation by each of these methods
  - (iii) Supplement conclusion with any additional information if available

## Going Concern and Non-Going Concern Valuation
- Going concern assumption assumes the enterprise will continue in operation for the foreseeable future with no intention or need to liquidate
- Non-Going Concern Valuation (Liquidation Valuation) is the net value realized after disposing of all assets and discharging all liabilities
- ⚠️ Nuance: Going-concern value of a firm is generally greater than its liquidation value because it considers future profitability, intangible assets, and goodwill allowing the acquired firm to charge a premium
- ⚠️ Nuance: Non-going concern has a lower valuation because liquidation implies laying off employees and creates a bad reputation among potential investors
- Decision Rule: Apply non-going concern valuation only when investors believe the firm has no longer value as a going concern

## Valuation of Distressed Companies
- Distress arises from too much debt (default risk) or inability to meet operating expenses
- Distressed companies are businesses likely to or already defaulted on debt but there may still be some value remaining in the instruments they hold
- ❌ Not suitable when: Conventional methods are used for distressed companies because:
  - (1) DCF requires terminal value based on infinite life and ever-growing cash flows which are irrelevant due to negative cash flows
  - (2) Estimating cash flows is difficult due to decline in revenues and bankruptcy risk where DCF would yield negative value for equity or firm
  - (3) Discount rates used in conventional methods reflect operationally sound firms and must be adjusted for probability of failure

### Modified Discounted Cash Flow Valuation
- Requires coming up with probability distributions for cash flows across all possible outcomes adjusted for likelihood of default
- Discount rates are adjusted using:
  - Updated debt to equity ratios and unlevered beta to estimate the cost of equity
  - Updated measures of default risk to estimate the cost of debt
  - ⚠️ Nuance: In case of inability to estimate the entire distribution use expected cash flow formula:
    - 🔢 Formula: Expected cash flow_t = Cash flow_t * (1 - Probability of distress_t)

### DCF Valuation + Distress Value
- Conventional DCF understates the value if the firm fails before stable growth and assets are sold in distress sale
- Distressed firm calculation steps:
  - (i) Value the business as a going concern looking at expected cash flows if it follows the path back to financial health
  - (ii) Determine the probability of distress over the lifetime of the DCF analysis
  - (iii) Estimate the distress sale value as a percentage of book value or going concern DCF value of equity
  - 🔢 Formula: Value of Equity = DCF value of equity * (1 - Probability of distress) + Distress sale value of equity * (Probability of distress)

### Adjusted Present Value (APV) Model
- Separates investment decisions from financing decisions by first computing unlevered firm value and then adjusting for debt effects
- 🔢 Formula: Firm Value = Unlevered Firm Value + (Tax Benefits of Debt - Expected Bankruptcy Cost from Debt)
- 🔢 Formula: Expected Bankruptcy Costs = (Unlevered firm value - Distress Sale Value) * Probability of Distress

### Relative Valuation for Distressed Firms
- Revenue and EBITDA multiples are more popular than Price Earnings or Price to Book Value because PE and PB often cannot be used for distressed firms
- ⚠️ Nuance: Multiples must be adjusted downward from the industry average (e.g., if industry average is 2 times revenues adjust down to 1.25 times for a distressed firm)

## Valuation of Start Ups
- Three common globally accepted methods:
  - (i) Earning/Cash Flow Approach (discounted foreseeable cash flows)
  - (ii) Asset Approach (current realizable value of assets and liabilities, used in non-going concern)
  - (iii) Market Approach (assigns value based on comparable companies in same/similar industries)
- ⚠️ Limitation: Traditional approaches pre-suppose a business that is established and generates cash flows using its assets
- ⚠️ Nuance: Startups are not established, cash flows are spent on marketing, and profitability is often a cursed word in startup investor circles
- ⚠️ Drawback: The most significant differentiator is that there is no historical data available to draw future projections and value rests entirely on future growth potential from untested ideas
- ❌ Not suitable when: Traditional methods are applied to new-age startups because:
  - (1) Income Approach fails because startups operate under the assumption of not generating positive cash flows in the foreseeable future
  - (2) Asset Approach fails because startups have negligible physical assets (most assets are IP and intangibles) and startups are going concerns so value should not be limited to liquidation value
  - (3) Market Approach fails because startups are disruptors functioning in markets without established competitors making numbers skewed and not comparable

### Value Drivers for Startups
- Product: Uniqueness and readiness (fully functional prototype attracts higher value than an idea), market testing, and customer responses
- Management: Credentials and balance of team (engineers, finance, MBAs). 📌 Note: More than half of Indian unicorn founders are from IIT/IIM, which is looked upon favorably by investors
- Traction: Quantifiable evidence that the product/service works and there is demand
- Revenue: Multiple revenue streams make the company more valuable (though not mandatory)
- Industry Attractiveness: Logistics, distribution, customer base impact sustainable scaling (e.g., tourism startup value drops if lockdowns are expected)
- Demand - Supply: Attractive industry creates higher investor demand making individual companies more valuable
- Competitiveness: Lesser competitors = more valuable, first-mover advantage

### Methods for Valuing Startups
- Berkus Approach:
  - Valuing based on detailed assessment of five success factors: (1) Basic value, (2) Technology, (3) Execution, (4) Strategic relationships, (5) Production and sales
  - ⚠️ Nuance: Caps pre-revenue valuations at $2 million and post-revenue valuations at $2.5 million
  - ⚠️ Limitation: Does not consider other market factors but useful as an uncomplicated tool
- Cost-to-Duplicate Approach:
  - Takes into account all costs and expenses associated with the startup and product development including physical asset purchases
  - ⚠️ Limitation: Criticized for not focusing on future revenue projections or intangible assets
- Comparable Transactions Method:
  - Precedent-based approach answering how much similar startups were valued at
  - 📎 Example Insight: Logistics startup XYZ acquired for 560 crores with 24 crore active users (~23 per user) can be used to value ABC Ltd with 1.75 crore users at ~40 crores
  - ⚠️ Nuance: Ratios or multipliers must be adjusted for differentiating factors like proprietary tech, intangibles, penetration, and location
- Scorecard Valuation Method:
  - Compares startup to others already funded with added qualities and weights:
    - Strength of team: 0-30%
    - Size of opportunity: 0-25%
    - Product or service: 0-15%
    - Competitive environment: 0-10%
    - Marketing, sales channels, and partnerships: 0-10%
    - Need for additional investment: 0-5%
    - Others: 0-5%
  - Comparison percentage is assigned: on par (100%), below (<100%), above (>100%) and multiplied by the weight
  - 📎 Example Insight: Marketing team gets 150% score where weight is 10% yielding a factor of 0.15
  - Sum of all factors is multiplied by average pre-money valuation of comparables in the sector to get pre-revenue valuation
- First Chicago Method:
  - Combines DCF and market approach across three scenarios: Worst-case, Normal-case, Best-case
  - Valuations of each are multiplied by a probability factor to arrive at a weighted average value
- Venture Capital Method:
  - Investors seek a return equal to a multiple of their initial investment (e.g., 10x, 20x, 30x) at exit or achieve a specific IRR
  - Post-money value is calculated by discounting the required rate of return

## Valuation of Digital Platforms
- Digital platform is a software-based online infrastructure facilitating interactions and transactions between users (many-to-many)
- Platform Categories:
  - Marketplace: Matches multiple buyers to multiple suppliers (e.g., Booking.com, Uber, Amazon)
  - Search Engine: Matches information seekers to information sources (e.g., Google, Bing, Baidu)
  - Repository: Suppliers deposit materials to be retrieved by users (e.g., Spotify, YouTube, GitHub)
  - Digital Communication: Users send messages/documents or interact in real-time (e.g., WhatsApp, Teams, Telegram, Slack)
  - Digital Community: Users remain virtually connected and interact (e.g., Facebook, LinkedIn)
  - Payments Platform: Matches those owing money and those wanting to be paid (e.g., Paytm, GPay)

### Income Approach for Digital Platforms
- Emphasizes projected financial performance using revenue/cost drivers, capex, and working capital cycles
- Top-Down Approach: Backward working starting with Total Addressable Market (TAM) -> Serviceable Addressable Market (SAM) -> Serviceable Obtainable Market (SOM)
  - ⚠️ Nuance: Revenue models differ by platform type (subscriber-based for repository vs transaction-based for payments)
  - ⚠️ Nuance: Platforms often use penetrative strategies by burning cash on books and keeping low margins to build market share and cash burn reduces over time
  - ⚠️ Limitation: Top-Down Approach can be too ambitious for nascent platforms because estimating market size and share poses practical challenges
- Bottom-Up Approach: Platforms estimate earnings based on limited resources and financial constraints allowing promoters to target high-margin sales and cost-cutting
- Discounting Rate:
  - Discount Free Cash Flow to Firm (FCFF) using WACC and Free Cash Flow to Equity (FCFE) using Cost of Equity (Ke) via CAPM
- Specific Considerations:
  - (a) Beta: Unique nature of platforms and scarcity of listed comparables makes estimating beta challenging requiring comparisons with diversified sectors or international peers
  - (b) Survival: Highly dependent on management quality, adaptability, and foresight
  - 📌 Note: Specific risks cannot be estimated using CAPM alone so Company Specific Risk Premium (CSRP) or Alpha must be added to the cost of equity
  - ⚠️ Nuance: CSRP is higher for nascent digital platforms compared to mature ones with large customer bases

### Market Approach for Digital Platforms
- Draws comparisons using multiples (P/E, EV/EBITDA, Price to Book, Price to Sales)
- ❌ Not suitable when: Normal multiples are used directly without adjustments because:
  - (1) Listed comparables are scarce or absent
  - (2) Profits and EBITDA may be negative for certain digital platforms
  - (3) Platforms are capital-lite making Book Value very low
- ✅ Implication: Lays emphasis on revenue multiples where comparison is sought on the primary driver of revenue
- Primary Revenue Drivers by Category:
  - Market Place: Number of bookings made, number of registered users, volume of transactions
  - Payment: Number of active subscribers, number of merchants registered, compatibility and speed of OS, security, ease of use
  - Community: Number of users, subscription fees, platform for professionals
  - Communication: Number of users, sponsored links, advertising revenue
  - Repository: Number of readers and contributors, authenticity of data, duration of use, quality and variety of data
  - Search: Number of users, relevant search results, time taken per search
  - 📎 Example Insight: Two search engines can be compared based on active users and average time for results where the faster/more relevant engine is valued at a premium
  - 📎 Example Insight: Repository compared based on users, average views per user, and average revenue per user

### Cost Approach for Digital Platforms
- Estimates value based on the sum total of the cost to build the platform (developer hours spent writing code)
- ⚠️ Limitation: Fails to take into account the revenue-generating capacity of the platform which creates significantly higher value

## Valuation of Professional/ Consultancy Firms
- Knowledge-based services (CAs, Advocates, Management Consultants) that vary significantly by nature of services
- Experts look at historical data to compare with industry KPIs and benchmarks using Audited Annual Statements and IT Returns
- Terminal Value (projected growth) impacts value but carries inherent risks as future earnings may not materialize
- ⚠️ Nuance: Normalisation of net income and cash flows is crucial to allow comparison on equal footing by adding back non-cash and specific items
- KPIs vary greatly and must align with the goals of the acquirer firm

## Impact of ESG on Valuation
- ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance) is a framework designed to be embedded in strategy to generate value for all stakeholders
- Factors under consideration:
  - Environmental: Climate change, water, waste generation, emissions, biodiversity
  - Social: Employee development, diversity & inclusion, community development, health & safety, customer
  - Governance: Board independence, board diversity, anti-corruption & bribery, tax transparency, ethical conduct
- ESG ratings influence fundraising where high focus leads to preferential lower cost of debt or access to green/sustainability bonds
- ⚠️ Nuance: Shift from "good to have" to "must to have" territory for organizational resiliency
- How to incorporate ESG risks in Valuation:
  - (1) Adjusting discount rate by including risk premium (more practical but less explicit)
  - (2) Adjusting cash flows (more explicit)
- Cash flow adjustment by factor:
  - (i) Environmental: Carry out 2-degree scenario analysis or adjust cash flows by considering carbon points
  - (ii) Social: Adjust for cost of social measures (better labor working conditions, CSR, welfare) on revenue
  - (iii) Governance: Adjust for the impact of poor governance on revenue in the form of penalties, fines, and taxes

## Case Studies

### Case Study 1: Vodafone merger with Idea Cellular Ltd.
- Valuation methods used:
  - (a) Market Value Method (NSE share price)
  - (b) Comparable Companies Market Multiple Method (BSE/NSE peers)
  - (c) NAV Method (asset based as of Dec 31, 2016)
- ⚠️ Nuance: DCF method was not used because managements did not provide projected cash flows
- 📌 Note: Valuation methods, even if technically correct, may not elicit positive stock impact due to unquantifiable perceived value (Idea Cellular fell after merger announcement due to perceived undervaluation)

### Case Study 2: Facebook acquisition of WhatsApp
- Facebook takeover of WhatsApp for $21.8 billion USD in 2015
- Influencing characteristics:
  - Free text-messaging with $1/yr fee and 450 million users
  - 70% of users were active
  - User growth of 1 million users a day leading to 1 billion users within a year
  - Gross per-user value came to $55 (included $4 billion employee retention payout)
  - Monetization plan via international messaging
